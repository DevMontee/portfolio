Project Overview

Vehicle suspension systems serve three critical functions: adapting to road disturbances for passenger comfort, maximizing tire road grip, and providing steering stability. This project focuses on designing and optimizing a PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) controller for a semi-active suspension system, which offers superior performance compared to passive systems while being more cost-effective and energy-efficient than fully active systems.

Key Objective

Design a PID controller for a semi-active suspension system that minimizes settling time and percent overshoot across different road conditions, improving upon the performance of conventional passive suspension systems through systematic parameter tuning and performance analysis.

Comparison of suspension system architectures: (a) Passive system with fixed damping, (b) Semi-active system with variable damper, (c) Active system with force actuator

Suspension System Comparison

Passive Suspension

Characteristics: Fixed spring stiffness and constant damping coefficient designed for specific operating conditions.

Limitations: Cannot adapt to varying road conditions or vehicle loading, resulting in compromised performance across different scenarios.

Advantages: Simple, reliable, low cost, no energy consumption.

Semi-Active Suspension

Characteristics: Variable damping coefficient adjusted in real-time through electronic control without adding external energy.

Benefits: Superior ride comfort compared to passive systems, significantly lower energy consumption and cost than active systems.

Application: Commonly found in modern commercial vehicles due to optimal cost-performance balance.

Active Suspension

Characteristics: Force actuators providing dynamic control across wide frequency ranges with external power input.

Performance: Best adaptation to road disturbances and superior ride quality.

Drawbacks: High complexity, significant energy consumption, increased vehicle weight, and substantially higher cost.

Mathematical Modeling

A quarter-car model with two degrees of freedom (sprung mass and unsprung mass) was developed to analyze suspension performance. Newton's second law was applied to derive state-space equations for both passive and semi-active configurations, with key assumptions including negligible roll and pitch motions, longitudinal axis symmetry, and tire damping and spring properties.

Model Parameters

Sprung Mass (Ms): Vehicle body mass | Unsprung Mass (Mu): Wheel assembly mass | Spring Coefficient (Ks): Suspension stiffness | Tire Spring Coefficient (Kt): Tire stiffness | Damping Coefficient (Cs/β): Variable damper controlled by PID | Road Disturbance: Zo(t) input function

MATLAB Simulink implementation of semi-active suspension with PID controller featuring manual tuning for different road profiles

The semi-active model replaces the fixed damper (Cs) with a variable coefficient (β) controlled by the PID feedback loop. Transfer functions were derived using Laplace transforms to establish the relationship between road disturbance input and sprung mass displacement output.

Road Disturbance Profiles

Three distinct road disturbance profiles were analyzed to evaluate controller performance across diverse driving conditions simulating real-world scenarios from smooth highways to severely damaged roads.

Profile 1: Sinusoidal road disturbance representing smooth undulating road surfaces or speed bumps
Profile 2: Step function disturbance simulating curb impacts or abrupt surface height changes
Profile 3: Consecutive impulse jerks representing severely damaged roads with potholes and irregular surfaces

PID Controller Tuning Methodology

Manual tuning was performed to optimize PID parameters (Kp, Ki, Kd) for each road profile. The PID controller was implemented through manual connection rather than using Simulink's built-in block due to the nonlinear nature of the ground displacement input. Tuning focused on minimizing settling time (Ts) and percent overshoot (PO) to maximize passenger comfort.

Tuning Strategy

Starting from literature-optimized baseline values, parameters were adjusted individually to observe their effects on system response. Proportional gain (Kp) adjusted response speed, integral gain (Ki) reduced steady-state error, and derivative gain (Kd) minimized overshoot and improved stability.

Optimized PID Parameters

Key Findings

Increasing Ki generally increased percent overshoot (undesirable for comfort), while increasing Kp improved response speed but required careful balancing. Derivative gain (Kd) effectively reduced overshoot but had minimal impact on certain road profiles. Statistical analysis confirmed improvements in settling time and time to rise for the controlled sprung mass, directly enhancing passenger comfort.

Technical Implementation

  • Developed quarter-car mathematical model with state-space representation
  • Derived transfer functions using Laplace transforms for passive and semi-active configurations
  • Implemented nonlinear control system in MATLAB/Simulink with manual PID connection
  • Performed systematic parameter tuning using trial-and-error methodology
  • Analyzed performance metrics: settling time, rise time, percent overshoot, steady-state error
  • Conducted statistical analysis of displacement signals for comparative evaluation
  • Validated improvements over passive baseline across all three road profiles
  • Documented comprehensive design rationale and optimization process

Performance Criteria Analysis

The primary performance metrics evaluated were settling time (Ts), rise time (Tr), percent overshoot (PO), and steady-state error (ess). For optimal ride comfort, minimizing settling time and percent overshoot is critical as these directly correlate to passenger perception of suspension quality and component wear.

Passive Suspension Baseline Results

Profile 1: Ts = 5.2s, Tr = 7.0s, PO = 2% | Profile 2: Ts = 5.1s, Tr = 5.5s, PO = 75% | Profile 3: Ts = 8.0s, Tr = 0.8s, PO = 80%

The semi-active system with optimized PID control demonstrated measurable improvements in settling characteristics and reduced displacement amplitude. Statistical analysis revealed decreased mean values for sprung mass displacement and improved response consistency across varying road conditions.

Conclusions & Impact

This project successfully demonstrated that semi-active suspension systems with properly tuned PID controllers offer substantial performance advantages over passive systems. Through systematic parameter optimization, improvements in settling time and rise time were achieved for the sprung mass, directly enhancing passenger comfort. The manual tuning methodology proved effective despite the nonlinear system characteristics, yielding profile-specific optimal gains that balanced response speed, stability, and steady-state accuracy.

Future Enhancements

With additional development time, analytical pole-matching techniques could be explored for more systematic controller design. Adaptive control strategies enabling real-time parameter adjustment based on detected road conditions would further improve performance. Integration with vehicle sensors for preview control and machine learning optimization of PID gains represent promising research directions.
